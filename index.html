Being Culture Neutral

To efficiently work in today’s global environment necessitates the ability to understand multi-cultural nuances and to help people manage potential conflicts caused by these differences…

Ever wondered, why cross cultural awareness and sensitivity, a key dimension in a Global Delivery organization? Why do we have our own views on culture? Why is culture just not about different countries? How are different approaches important in our daily communications? How can we increase our level of intercultural sensitivity? How can we expand our thinking and enrich our work and personal lives by applying new awareness through communication?

Certainly, culture is not an inbred characteristic, but shaped by what we learn in the context of our social group or category. Culture is specific to an individual and group/category. It is very often a complex pattern of ideas, emotions and observable behaviors that tend to be expected, reinforced and rewarded by and within a particular group.

As IBM’s delivery organization extends itself to working world wide, we foresee the need for practitioners to work with colleagues and clients in a universal environment. Definitely, one needs to be clear, prepared, simple, vivid, natural and concise in interactions.  Communication is more important than language, and plays a vital role. One needs to be observant and look into other cultures’ customs and body language, while perfecting political abilities to meet intercultural management requirements.

Cross culture awareness and sensitivity refers to the basic ability of people within the business to recognize, interpret and correctly react to people, incidences or situations that are open to misunderstanding due to cultural differences. What stimulates this is a cross cultural knowledge as the learner understands and appreciates a culture internally. Being sensitive, is a natural by-product of awareness and refers to the ability to read into situations, contexts and behaviors that are culturally rooted and being able to react to them appropriately.

Often, the key elements that we notice at the outset include language, food, architecture, physical contact, dress, literature, climate, noise, pace of life, public emotion, work ethics, music, infrastructure which are some of the key visible areas. However, culture is much more than this. It is defined as a shared system of values leading to a dynamic process for solving human problems.

As the world shrinks to a global village, today’s executive must adapt well to new people, cultures, places, situations. The leaders must understand the company’s global strategy and able to transform it into locally meaningful actions, which are supported by the local staff and accepted by the global management. This requires keeping the global standards and practices, but realizing the need for localization. They also need to understand that his/her culture’s way of doing things may not always the best, which calls for a more open, and tolerant approach.

Sailing Smart amidst Diverse Cultures:

  • Avoid expressive, enthusiastic behavior.
  • Prepare well for the meetings. Then it is easier to stick to neutral topics.
  • Become an expert in body language and look for subtle signs.
  • Begin all reports with an executive summary
  • Management is a continuously improving process.
  • Consider the whole picture before you judge…and when you judge, be sure of what you are doing.
  • End all reports with a concluding overview.
  • Satisfaction comes from improved relationships with clients and colleagues.
  • Softness, persistence, politeness and patience go a long way.
  • Give people time to work through conflicts.
  • Achieve congruence among various goals.

What makes a person a Cross Cultural Expert?

  • Has an explorer’s mind.
  • Speaks well, but listens even better.
  • Communicates well face-to-face.
  • Can understand what the people are not saying.
  • Enjoys traveling and living overseas.
  • Has excellent foreign language skills.
  • Doesn’t spend all the time with his/her own expatriate community, likes to mingle with foreign colleagues and friends.
  • Understands and is proud of her/his culture, values, history and family.
